# Mini-project report

## Who Would We Miss?

This mini-project tests whether Bayesian risk targeting can reduce missed high-risk households when support coverage is limited.

## Data used

The analysis uses SIHBS 2022 household microdata modules. The household sample contains **7,212 households** across **17 regions**.

## Descriptive findings

| Indicator | Weighted percent |
|---|---:|
| Climate/livelihood shock exposure | 71.9% |
| Moderate/severe food insecurity proxy | 44.1% |
| Formal support received after shock | 3.8% |
| High-risk unreached outcome | 40.1% |

## Targeting result at 20% support coverage

| Strategy | High-risk unreached reached | High-risk unreached missed |
|---|---:|---:|
| Bayesian risk targeting | 34.0% | 66.0% |
| Climate/livelihood shock-only | 27.8% | 72.2% |
| Drought-only | 29.1% | 70.9% |
| Poverty-only | 19.5% | 80.5% |
| Region-burden targeting | 28.8% | 71.2% |
| Direct-needs benchmark | 46.7% | 53.3% |
| Random targeting | 20.0% | 80.0% |

The direct-needs benchmark uses post-impact need information and should be interpreted as an upper-bound comparison, not as an early targeting rule.

## Interpretation

Bayesian targeting creates a probability-based ranking of households likely to be both affected and unreached, while accounting for regional differences. The strongest public message is:

> Early warning should not stop at identifying where shocks happen. It should help identify who is likely to suffer, who is least likely to be reached, and how limited support can be targeted more fairly.
