A climate shock map can tell us where a hazard occurred.

But it may not tell us who will be missed by the response.

So I built a Bayesian climate shock-to-action targeting analysis using SIHBS 2022 household data from Somalia.

The question was simple:

**If emergency support can reach only a limited share of households, which targeting rule misses the fewest high-risk unreached households?**

I defined high-risk unreached households as those that experienced a climate/livelihood shock, showed food-security stress or erosive coping, and reported no formal support from government, local NGOs, or international organizations.

The analysis found:

• 71.9% of households were exposed to climate/livelihood shocks.  
• 44.1% met the moderate/severe food insecurity proxy.  
• Only 3.8% reported formal support after shock.  
• 40.1% were classified as high-risk and unreached.

Then I compared targeting rules: shock-only, drought-only, poverty-only, region-burden targeting, random targeting, a direct-needs benchmark, and Bayesian risk targeting.

The key output is a “Missed Households Curve,” showing how many high-risk unreached households remain invisible when support coverage is limited.

At 20% support coverage, Bayesian risk targeting reached 34.0% of high-risk unreached households, compared with 27.8% under climate/livelihood shock-only targeting and 19.5% under poverty-only targeting.

For me, the message is clear:

Early warning should not stop at identifying where shocks happen.

It should help us understand who is likely to suffer, who is least likely to be reached, and how limited support can be targeted more fairly.

This is the direction I believe climate data analysis should move toward:

**From risk mapping to action intelligence.**
